King's Court

Objective

The goal in King's Court is to be the player with the most points at the end of the game.

How to Play

King’s Court utilizes a deck of 52 cards – A King of each suit, and four suits of 1-6 cards in pairs (two red threes, two blue sixes, etc). The game also makes use of a 7x7 board, 49 spaces, the direct center of which will be reserved for a King card, 12 spaces are filled with randomly selected 1-6 cards at the beginning of the game.


A standard new game with an "outside border" layout. The blue 0 in the middle is the King. The player has six cards in their hand.

A standard new game with a "snowflake" layout. The green 0 in the middle is the King. The player has six cards in their hand.

The game starts with an empty board with twelve tiles arranged in pre-determined layout styles with a King card layed in the direct center of the King’s Court, the other King cards should be set aside for the remainder of the game. The cards are all random along with the King’s suit. This leaves 36 cards that will be dealt out to the players in rotation until each player has six cards with any remaining cards, unrevealed, in a stack. Each player starts the game with 0 points for a Total Score. A player is randomly selected to start the game and proceeding moves made in a cyclical rotation.

The player that starts places one card from their hand on the board (see: Making a Move), then adds their Round Score (see: Scoring) to their Total Score. If there are cards in the withdraw stack, the player will then draw another card for their hand to replace the one that was put on the board. Following plays/scoring will be made by the other players in clockwise rotation, eventually returning to the original first player who repeats the process.

The game ends when all cards are on the board or no additional plays can be made. The player with the most points at the end of the game wins.

Making a Move

Each time a player's turn comes up, the player has to place a card in an empty spot on the board. Beyond scoring, there are other rules that must be considered when a player reaches their turn.

Not all open spaces are legally playable. A move is only legal if:

  1. The empty spot has no neighboring cards (left, right, above, below) with the same VALUE as card/tile being placed *


    2. The space with the white circle can have any card value placed in it.

    The space with the white circle cannot have a 2 or a 6 card placed in it.

    The space with the white circle cannot have a 3,5 or 6 card placed in it.

  2. Blue spaces on board can only be filled in once the surrounding green spaces have been filled in by cards


The green check indicates the only playable blue space at that moment.

* The exception to this is mercy rule which allows this only if the player is down to one card and no spots are legally playable. If player cannot make a move, the next player in rotation takes their turn.

Scoring

There are many different ways to earn points in King's Court that are all dependent on the empty spot where the player lays a card during their turn. Ideally, a player's objective is to try and make high scoring plays while also not giving his/her opponents a higher scoring opportunity. There are many variables to consider and there often isn't a "perfect move"; a single card can affect over a dozen spaces in a single move.

The King's Purpose

The King is the card in the middle of the board represented with either "K" or "0". The King's suit/color determines the Prime Suit. The Prime Suit's role is applied in Column and Row Scoring. The King has a card value of 10 which is applied in the Rule of Sevens, Chain Scoring, and special instances of Column/Row Scoring

The blue 0 (King) in the middle of the board indicates the Prime Suit is Blue

The green 0 (King) in the middle of the board indicates the Prime Suit is Green

Rule of Sevens

The single most important scoring rule is the Rule of Sevens. The Rule of Sevens is described as so:

  1. If a player's Sum Total for a move LANDS ON the current Checkpoint (7, 14, 21, 28) of the played space, the player adds the checkpoint's value to their score. (See: fig RO7-I)
  2. If a player's Sum Total for a move FALLS BELOW the current Checkpoint of the played space, no points are earned via the Rule of Sevens, but additional scoring combinations still apply. (See: fig RO7-II)
  3. If a player's Sum Total for a move EXCEEDS the Checkpoint of the played space, no points are earned via the Rule of Sevens, and additional scoring in current round is divided by 2 and rounded down to nearest whole number. (See: fig RO7-III)

The Rule of Sevens is based on the four side spaces (left, right, up, down) surrounding the empty spot a player is placing their card. Players must find the sum of the card values surrounding the empty space to the immediate left, right, up, and down of said space. This is called the Space Value. When a player makes a move, the player will add the value of their card to the Space Value to create the Sum Total.

The Sum Total is important. Ideally, a player wants a sum total of a multiple of seven. The four possible multiples of seven (7, 14, 21, 28) are called the Checkpoints. If a player's sum total hits any of the checkpoints, the player gains the checkpoint's value in points in addition to other possible scoring combos. If the Sum Total does not land on a checkpoint, no points are earned from the Rule of Sevens, though additional scoring still applies.

If the Sum Total goes beyond the next checkpoint for that space, the Round Score for that player is cut in half and rounded down to the nearest whole number. If a space has a Space Value of 5, the checkpoint is 7. If the player puts a 4 down, the sum total becomes 9, going beyond 7. The player’s penalty is halving their Round Score. If they score 24 points in score combos, their round score becomes 24/2 = 12. If the player scored 37 points in score combos, the round score is 37/2 = 18.5 rounded down to 18.

Column and Row Scoring

There are two ways to score points based on the column and row a player places his card.

  1. A player gains their card's value in points for every card of equal value in the same row/column of the played space.

    ie. The top-left corner of the board is empty, there is a blue 5 and a red 5 in the top row, and a green 5 in the left-most column. If a player places the other green 5 in the top left corner of the board, the player would gain 5 (card value) x 3 = 15 points in addition to other potential scoring combinations.

Diagonal/Chain Scoring

The last type of scoring is based on the cards/tiles touching the corners of the empty space the player chooses to play their card. There are no rules restricting which cards can touch the corner of the card that's in play, cards of the same value can touch on corners unlike cards in the Side Spaces (left, right, up, down) touching the space being played. The corners are where Chain Scoring comes into play. The important rule to remember is that a Chain starts on any corner of the empty space a player makes their move, and all cards in a chain must be in a straight line connected at the immediate corners. There are two ways to earn points through chaining:

  1. If a series of cards are chained by their VALUE at the corner of an empty spot and the player lays a card of EQUAL VALUE, the player earns the card's value of points per every card in the chain. (See: COR-I)

  2. If a series of cards are chained by their COLOR at the corner of an empty spot and the player lays a card of EQUAL SUIT/COLOR, the player earns points equal to the VALUES SHOWN ON EACH CARD OF THE COLOR CHAIN. (See: COR-II)

  3. If a card of EQUAL VALUE AND COLOR to the card being played is chained onto the corner, points are stacked for VALUE and COLOR chaining. (See: COR-III)

Appendix I: Terms

Card Color

See: Card Suit


Card Suit

The one of the four colors of cards (by default: red, green, blue, and yellow)


Card Value

The value of a card is equivalent to the number on the card itself.


Checkpoint

The first multiple of seven (7, 14, 21, 28) beyond the current Space Value of an empty space.

ie. if a Space Value = 4, the checkpoint on that space is 7. If a Space Value = 15, the checkpoint on that space is 21. If a Space Value = 7, the checkpoint on that space is 14.


Empty Space/Spot

An unoccupied space on the gameboard where no card has been played yet.


King

The card in the center of the board (either 'K' or '0') with a value of 10. Also determines the Prime Suit


King's Court

The blue and green squares around the King in the center. The blue squares can only be played when the surrounding green Side Spaces have been filled in.


Prime Card

Any card that matches the current games Prime Suit

Prime Suit

The card suit determined by the color/suit of the King card in the middle of the board.


Round (Turn)

A round is the moment a player has control over the board, is allowed to make their move, and allowed to take their score. As soon as a player takes their score for the card they recently placed, the player’s round is over and the next player’s round begins.


Round Score

The score the player makes during their round which is the sum of points earned from Rule of Sevens, Column/Row, and Diagonal/Corner scores. Penalties from breaking the Rule of Sevens only apply to this score. Round scores are added together to make the player's Total Score.


Rule of Sevens

  • If a player's Sum Total for a move LANDS ON a Checkpoint, the player adds the checkpoint's value to their score.
  • If a player's Sum Total FALLS BELOW the Checkpoint, no points are earned via the Rule of Sevens, but additional scoring combinations still apply.
  • If a player's Sum Total for a move EXCEEDS the Checkpoint of the played space, no points are earned via the Rule of Sevens, and additional scoring in current round (Round Score) is divided by 2 and rounded down to nearest whole number.

Side Spaces

Around any Empty Space, the side spaces are the four spaces to the immediate left, right, up, and down of the said empty space. See figures in Space Value for example of side spaces.


Space Value

The value of an Empty Space based on the sum of the card values in the occupied Side Spaces of the said empty space.

Space value of 3+3+5+6=17. The white cross shows the Side Spaces for the given Empty Space
Space value of 6+3=9. The white cross shows the Side Spaces for the given Empty Space. Empty Side Spaces and Side Spaces that don't exist (anything beyond the board) are considered 0 values.
Space value of all open spaces on example board. Note the "0" (King) has a value of 10.


Sum Total

The sum of the Space Value of an empty spot plus the value of the card the player is putting down on it.

Total Score

The sum score for all the player's round scores. The player with the largest Total Score at the end of the game wins.
